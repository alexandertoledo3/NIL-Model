Why the law score should matter (Step 16): Restrictive state NIL rules can (1) raise transaction costs for deals, (2) limit institutional facilitation through collectives and school marketing, (3) slow deal completion through review requirements, (4) reduce collective flexibility in structuring compensation, and (5) weaken the alignment between player value and market compensation. Each of these channels should increase the gap between observed NIL and what performance and exposure variables predict, which is the core prediction tested here.

Coding memo
Each component is coded 0 (absent/weak) or 1 (present/restrictive) based on NCPA rankings, Student Press Law Center state-by-state guide, and On3 state legislation database. Equal-weight version sums all five. Weighted version gives 2× weight to school involvement and collective restrictions — the two components with the clearest economic channel through reduced institutional facilitation. Binary version flags states at ≥3 on the equal-weight score. All three versions enter separate regressions as robustness on the law-score construction.

Performance formula weights (documented)
QB: PassYds + PassTD×100 + RushYds + RushTD×100 − INT×50. TDs and INTs weighted heavily because they are the terminal events of drives — disproportionate impact on game outcomes per EPA literature.
RB: RushYds + RushTD×100 + RecYds + RecTD×100 + Rec×8. Receptions weighted 8 because modern RB pass-catching usage is independently valued.
WR: RecYds + RecTD×100 + Rec×10. Receptions weighted 10 because target share signals a distinct marketable trait.
All values z-scored within position to make QB, RB, and WR comparable. Alternative weightings (TD×60, TD×150, no INT penalty) tested — direction of main result stable in all cases.
SchoolBrand index definition
Each school assigned a brand score 1–5 based on the standardized sum of (a) average AP poll finish over the prior 5 years and (b) average home attendance. Index is then rescaled to 1–5 range. Formal implementation: SchoolBrand = z(AP_5yr_avg) + z(Attendance_5yr_avg), then rescaled. This replaces the previous more-subjective school-brand coding.

Methods section — paper-ready language
Sample selection & data construction

Team selection. The sample consists of top-5 SEC and top-5 ACC football programs based on final regular-season conference standings for each year from 2023 through 2024, with 2025 included as an exploratory supplement. Team-seasons were selected by conference standings rather than preseason rankings to avoid selection on expectations; final standings reflect on-field performance and the associated NIL market dynamics that are the focus of this study.

Player selection. Within each team-season, all starting quarterbacks, notable backup quarterbacks with meaningful snap counts, the top two to three running backs by carries, and the top three to five wide receivers by receiving yards were included. This produces a balanced representation of the offensive skill positions most visible in the NIL market. Defensive players and offensive linemen were excluded because their NIL valuations are systematically different and would require a separate empirical strategy.

Treatment of 2025. Because 2025 season data and NIL valuations remain subject to revision, 2025 observations are reported only in the exploratory supplement and are not part of the main analysis sample. The main results cover the 2023 and 2024 seasons.

NIL valuation verification. Each player's NIL valuation was obtained from On3 public reports (on3.com/nil/rankings) where available, and supplemented with reporting from CollegeFootballNetwork and FOX Sports NIL coverage. Each valuation is labeled as verified (directly cited in public reporting) or estimated (interpolated from comparable players where no direct report was available), and the date of data collection is recorded for each observation. Robustness checks using only the verified-NIL subsample return directionally consistent results.

State law coding. Each state's NIL law was coded on five binary dimensions (disclosure requirement, school involvement restriction, collective restriction, contract review requirement, trademark restriction), synthesized from the NCPA state NIL rankings, the Student Press Law Center state-by-state guide, the On3 state legislation database, and the Business of College Sports legislative tracker. Three versions of the aggregate state law score are constructed: an equal-weight 0–5 sum, a weighted score giving double weight to school involvement and collective restrictions, and a binary high-regulation indicator coded 1 when the equal-weight score is at least 3.

Estimation. The empirical strategy is a two-stage observational regression. Stage 1 predicts log(NIL valuation) from standardized performance, recruiting star rating, team wins, playoff participation, ranked status, position, conference, starter status, school brand, all-conference recognition, high-visibility status, and year fixed effects. Expected NIL is the exponentiated fitted value from Stage 1. Inefficiency is defined three ways: absolute gap, log gap, and standardized gap relative to Expected NIL. Stage 2 regresses each inefficiency measure on the state law score and the full set of controls, reporting coefficients with OLS-based standard errors. For final publication, standard errors should be computed with heteroskedasticity-robust or school-clustered formulas using statistical software such as R, Stata, or SPSS.

Limitations — paper-ready paragraph

This analysis has several limitations. First, NIL valuations are imperfect proxies for true market compensation; On3's proprietary algorithm approximates market value but cannot observe every deal, particularly compensation from collectives. A subset of players in the sample have estimated rather than publicly reported valuations; robustness checks using only the verified-NIL subsample return directionally consistent results.

Second, the StateLawScore coding simplifies a complex and evolving legal environment. States update NIL statutes frequently, and the coding reflects rules in effect during each season. Alternative weighted and binary specifications are tested and results are stable across all three.

Third, Expected NIL is a benchmark constructed from observable player characteristics, not a direct measure of true market value. Deviations from Expected NIL may reflect genuine market inefficiency, unobserved legitimate variation (personality, off-field marketability), or measurement error in NIL valuations.

Fourth, the observational research design cannot establish causation. Associations between StateLawScore and inefficiency are consistent with the hypothesis that fragmented state rules affect market alignment, but cannot rule out selection on unobservables — for example, recruits with stronger independent brands may sort toward less-regulated states, creating apparent inefficiency concentrated in regulated states.

Fifth, the sample focuses on top-5 SEC and ACC teams. Findings may not generalize to lower-tier programs, other conferences, or non-revenue sports.

Sixth, standard errors reported in the accompanying dashboard are OLS-based homoskedastic approximations. For final publication, standard errors should be computed with heteroskedasticity-robust or school-clustered formulas using statistical software.
